Visa types for working in Japan

You can only legally work in Japan with a visa whose status of residence permits paid employment. The most common categories for foreign professionals:

VisaWho it's forKey requirements
Engineer / Specialist in Humanities / International Services Tech, finance, business, language teaching, design, marketing, translation. Bachelor's degree in a relevant field OR 10+ years of work experience in the field. Job offer required.
Highly Skilled Professional (HSP) High earners and senior specialists. Points-based (income, education, age, experience). 70+ points → 5-year visa, permanent residency in 3 years. 80+ → PR in 1 year.
Business Manager Founders / executives of a Japan-registered business. Real office, ¥5M capital, business plan, at least one full-time local hire (or equivalent investment).
Instructor Teachers at elementary, junior high, or high schools. Bachelor's degree + teaching credential.
Working Holiday Citizens of partner countries, age 18–30 (some 18–25). Bilateral agreement, time-limited (6–12 months), restricted to certain industries.
Designated Activities (J-Find) Recent graduates of top-ranked global universities looking for work in Japan. Up to 2 years to find employment.
Sponsorship: Most employers will sponsor your visa, but smaller companies sometimes will not. Filter for Apply from abroad jobs on the Jobs board, those are explicitly open to overseas candidates.

Japanese language levels & what they mean for hiring

JLPT (日本語能力試験) is the standard certification. Most job listings will refer to one of these levels, knowing what each actually means helps you self-assess honestly.

LevelWhat it really meansRoles you'll see require this
N1 / Native / Fluent Comfortable handling all business communication, formal documents, meetings, idioms. Senior / customer-facing / sales / management roles at domestic Japanese companies.
N2 / Business Can hold meetings in Japanese, read business emails, handle most workplace situations. Many tech, design, finance, and marketing roles at Japanese companies.
N3 / Conversational Daily-life conversation, basic workplace small talk, simple emails. Some engineering and creative roles where English is the team language.
N4–N5 / Basic Survival level. Asking for directions, ordering food, basic introductions. Roles that explicitly say "no Japanese required".
Not Required Team operates entirely in English. Common at international startups, large multinationals. Filter the board for jobs that don't need Japanese.
Reality check: Job listings often over-state the Japanese level required. Apply even if you're one level below, many roles have flexible thresholds for the right candidate.

Japanese resume formats

Japan uses two distinct resume documents, different from a Western CV. You'll typically submit both:

履歴書 (rirekisho)

A standardized personal-history document. Format is fixed: name + furigana, photo, date of birth, address, education and work history in chronological order, certifications, motivation, personal wishes. Hand-written used to be expected; digital is now common.

Build one with our Resume Builder.

職務経歴書 (shokumu keirekisho)

Your career-history document, closer to a Western CV. Tells the story of what you did at each company, projects, technologies, results. Less rigid format. Typically 2 pages.

Things to get right

  • Photo: ID-style, 3 cm × 4 cm, neutral background, taken within 3 months.
  • Address: Write it in Japanese order (大きい→小さい): postal code, prefecture, city, ward, building.
  • Dates: Use either Western years (西暦) or Japanese era years (和暦), but be consistent throughout the document.
  • Education: Include high school (高校), not just university.
  • Submission: Submit as PDF unless the company says otherwise.

Interview etiquette

  • Arrive 5–10 minutes early, not earlier, not exactly on time.
  • Business attire: dark suit (black, navy, charcoal), white shirt, conservative tie. This is the default even at "casual" Japanese companies for first interviews.
  • Bow on entry and exit. 15° is plenty unless meeting the company president.
  • Business cards (名刺): if you have them, present with both hands, kanji side up. Receive with both hands, study briefly, place on the table in front of you (not in your pocket).
  • Don't sit until invited.
  • Group interviews are common. Address the most senior person but speak to all.
  • Salary discussion typically happens after they've decided to make an offer, not in the first interview.
  • Send a thank-you email within 24 hours.

Salary expectations & negotiation

Japan's salary norms vary wildly by industry and company size. Rough 2026 ballparks for foreigner-friendly roles in Tokyo:

Role / levelAnnual base (¥)Notes
English Teacher (eikaiwa, ALT)2.8M – 4MEntry, often ¥250K/month.
Junior Engineer (1–3 yrs)4M – 6MHigher at gaishikei / foreign-cap startups.
Mid Engineer (3–7 yrs)6M – 10MMercari, SmartNews, etc. pay top of range.
Senior Engineer (7+ yrs)10M – 16MSometimes 20M+ at FAANG-equivalents.
Engineering Manager / Staff14M – 22MHighly variable; small companies pay less.
Sales / Business / Marketing (mid)6M – 12MOften has variable comp (commission).
Annual = base × 12 + bonus. Most Japanese companies pay 2× per year in summer and winter bonuses, typically totalling 2–6 months' base salary. Listings usually show base only, so always confirm bonus structure before signing.

Negotiation

  • Negotiation is less aggressive than in the US, but it happens. The norm is to ask for 10–15% above the offer, citing a competing offer or market data.
  • Be prepared to justify with numbers. "I earn $X now and need $Y to relocate" is more effective than "Industry standard is higher."
  • Non-cash items are often negotiable: signing bonus, relocation, remote-work days, training budget.

Useful Japanese for interviews

本日はよろしくお願いいたします。 Honjitsu wa yoroshiku onegai itashimasu. "Thank you for meeting with me today" (opening).
失礼いたします。 Shitsurei itashimasu. "Excuse me", when entering or leaving the room.
自己紹介させていただきます。 Jiko shōkai sasete itadakimasu. "Please allow me to introduce myself."
~の経験があります。 ~no keiken ga arimasu. "I have experience in ~"
御社で働きたい理由は… Onsha de hatarakitai riyū wa… "My reason for wanting to work at your company is…"
本日はお時間をいただき、ありがとうございました。 Honjitsu wa o-jikan o itadaki, arigatō gozaimashita. "Thank you for your time today" (closing).

Red flags to watch for

  • "Family-like atmosphere": often code for poor work-life balance and unpaid overtime.
  • Vague salary ("based on experience" / "negotiable"): sometimes legitimate, but request a written range before interviewing.
  • Resignation difficulty clauses: Japanese labour law lets you quit with 14 days' notice. Contracts demanding more should be flagged.
  • Excessive social events / drinking expectations: declining ought to be acceptable. If it isn't, the culture won't work for many foreigners.
  • "Service overtime" (サービス残業): unpaid overtime. Illegal but widespread; ask explicitly during interviews how overtime is handled.
  • No clear performance review cycle: companies that can't explain how raises and promotions work usually don't have them.
